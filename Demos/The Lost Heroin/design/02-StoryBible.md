# 02 — Story Bible

## The truth (spoiler-complete, for the team only)

Lanternquay is a harbor town under a lighthouse called the **Ember Light**.
Mara's mother is the keeper. Her father crews a fishing boat in the autumn
fleet. On the night of the **Great Storm**, the fleet — and the passenger liner
*Aurelia*, diverted toward harbor by the weather — were coming in on the light.

The mother went down to the water to help launch the rescue boats. She left
Mara, for the first time ever, to keep the lamp: *feed the wick, wind the
clockwork, keep it turning.* The storm terrified Mara. She hid under the table
in the lamp room and covered her ears. The clockwork ran down. The light went
out. The fleet scattered on the rocks; the Aurelia went down in the deep
channel; the rescue boats never came back. And behind the storm came the wave.

The game begins inside the instant the wave breaks over Lanternquay. Time has
stopped — not metaphorically: the entire playable world is that one held
second, dilated into a landscape. The "vanished sea" is the sea mid-leap. The
dry seabed is the water withdrawn into the wave, the way the shore empties
before a tsunami lands. The geography Mara walks is stitched from the sea's
memory of real places — some she knows, some belonging to the drowned. The
**Ghost Tide** that answers her feelings is the sea itself, conscious the way a
held breath is conscious, waiting for the keeper's daughter to do the thing
that lets the moment complete: **light the lamp**. Not to save anyone — it is
one second too late for that, forever — but so the wave may fall, the drowned
may stop falling, and the night may finally become the past.

Mara does not remember any of this when she wakes. Neither does the player.
They discover it together, in the same order, through the environment.

## Beginning / middle / ending

**Beginning (Prologue–Ch2).** Mara wakes in a tilted stilt-house in
Brinehollow, a village she doesn't know. The sea is gone. Doors stand open;
meals sit unfinished; long lines of footprints lead off the porches, down the
ladders, and out across the mud — everyone walked *toward* the sea. On the
horizon: the Standing Wave, a silver ridge that doesn't move. She follows the
footprints because there is nothing else alive to follow. The world is quiet,
strange, and — in the Salt Meadows — briefly, achingly beautiful. First hints
that the world responds to her: coral polyps glow when she hums; rain pools
reflect a sky that is *storming*, though the real sky is still.

**Middle (Ch3–Ch5).** The world deepens and darkens. In the Coral Forest she
learns the Call properly and meets the **Warden** — a pale whale that swims
through the *air*, high in the parallax, appearing at every threshold like a
witness. The Trench takes everything away: light, tide, the Warden. Something
lives in the dark — the **Undertow**, a current of black water that hunts
sound and fear. She crosses the Trench floor past the wreck-field of the
fishing fleet (her father's boat among them, marked only by a name-board the
player can read if they look). Climbing out, she enters the *Aurelia*, hanging
broken across the trench lip. Inside: luggage tagged for Lanternquay, a
tilted ballroom, cabins full of interrupted lives — and in one cabin, a child's
drawing of a lighthouse with a girl and two adults, signed with a wobbly **M**.
The player now knows where the ship was going and who was waiting for it. The
mid-game reveal closes question 1 by sight: from the Aurelia's bow, the
Standing Wave fills the sky, and it is unmistakably *a wave*, frozen — fish
hang inside it like flies in amber.

**Ending (Ch6–Finale).** At the wave's foot lies Lanternquay itself, caught
mid-instant: gulls nailed to the air, a dog mid-bark, laundry rigid in a wind
that isn't blowing, doors half-open, a bell tipped mid-swing. Nothing here can
be pushed or climbed the normal way — everything is locked in time — so Mara's
matured Call is the only tool: it lets one frozen thing at a time *finish its
motion*. She releases her path through the town (a cart completes its roll, a
mast completes its fall) and reaches the Ember Light. Inside, the past thaws
one floor at a time: sounds of that night bleed through, the storm grows on the
windows as she climbs, and in the lamp room she finds what the whole game has
been circling — a small girl-shaped shadow hiding under the table, hands over
its ears. Her. The player walks Mara to the shadow; kneeling beside it is the
game's only mandatory non-traversal input. The shadow stands, takes her hand,
and dissolves into her. Then, together with the sea — every ghost-light of the
drowned rising through the town to lend their glow — she lights the lamp with
the full Call.

Time resumes. The wave falls. The player is not taken out of control: they walk
Mara along the lighthouse gallery as the ocean closes over the world in
terrible, gentle slow motion. Then: blue. Lanternquay underwater, lit by the
burning lamp — impossibly still burning — and the drowned rising past her like
lanterns, upward, released. The Warden swims by, finally in water, enormous
and calm. Mara rises too — the sea carries her — and breaks the surface at
dawn. The sea is back. The horizon is empty and ordinary. The last shot: the
Ember Light above the waterline, turning, tended; a small figure in the
gallery; gulls. **She keeps the light now.**

## World history (for consistency, never for exposition)

- Lanternquay has kept the Ember Light for six generations; keepers are always
  women of Mara's line. The lamp's clockwork must be wound every four hours in
  storm season — a duty children are taught early. (Seen in: instructional
  scrimshaw plates in the lighthouse, a child-height practice crank.)
- Brinehollow was a summer oyster-village, culturally "downhill" of
  Lanternquay; its tide-bells are hand-me-downs from the lighthouse chandlery.
  (Seen in: matching foundry marks.)
- The Aurelia was not scheduled for Lanternquay; storm rerouting brought her.
  (Seen in: a corrected chalk route-board on the bridge.)
- The sea in this world has always been treated as an entity — offerings on
  keel-laying days, bells rung *to* it, not just about it. (Seen in: shrine
  niches with cups of salt, bell inscriptions "FOR THE LISTENER BELOW".)
  This is why the Ghost Tide answering a person reads, in-world, as folklore
  come true rather than a game mechanic.

## Symbolism ledger

- **The lamp / light:** duty, attention, "being awake for others". Every light
  in the game is dead until Mara wakes it — she is re-learning to keep light.
- **Bells:** voices for the voiceless; each bell rung is someone answered.
- **The Warden (air-whale):** the sea's patience. It never helps and never
  threatens; it *witnesses*. It appears at every act boundary. It only enters
  water in the finale — when the sea exists again.
- **The Undertow:** not a monster — the guilt. It hunts noise and panic and is
  dissolved by steady light. It is Mara-sized shadow when finally cornered
  (one silhouette frame, never repeated).
- **Footprints:** the dead walking to the sea = the moment everyone was taken;
  Mara walking the same direction = choosing to face it.
- **Hands:** the contextual verb is "reach". The final puzzle input is
  kneeling and taking a hand. The whole verb-set converges on touch.
- **Salt:** preserved grief; the Meadows are the night's tears, dried.

## Major reveals (order and delivery)

1. **The Standing Wave is a wave** — delivered by camera only: the Aurelia bow
   shot (end Ch5). No text.
2. **Lanternquay is frozen mid-instant** — delivered by walking into it (Ch6).
3. **The light was Mara's watch** — delivered by the under-table shadow and the
   practice crank; the player assembles it. No flashback movie: the lighthouse
   *thaws* sound only (wind, the clockwork slowing, a mother's voice calling
   one name from below — the only human voice in the game, wordless-then-name).
4. **Letting the wave fall is the goal** — the game never says it; the lamp is
   simply the only thing left to do, and the world has taught the player that
   light finishes things.

## Emotional arc (player-side)

unease → loneliness → wonder → awe → dread → sorrow → recognition ("it was
her") → resolve → release → warmth. The feelings system states (Still, Wonder,
Fear, Grief, Resolve) are the mechanical shadow of this arc; doc 03 maps them
beat by beat.

## What is deliberately never answered

- Whether the epilogue is "real", afterlife, or memory — the image is warm and
  the light is kept; that is enough.
- What the Warden is.
- Any character's name besides Mara's (and hers appears only as a wobbly "M").
